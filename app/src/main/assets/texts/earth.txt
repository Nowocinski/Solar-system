<p>
    <b>Earth</b> is the third <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and the only <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> known to
    harbor <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. While large <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_lakes_and_seas_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of largest lakes and seas in the Solar System">volumes of water</a> can be found throughout
    the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, only <a href="/wiki/Water_distribution_on_Earth" title="Water distribution on Earth">Earth sustains liquid surface water</a>. About 71% of Earth's surface is made
    up of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, dwarfing Earth's polar ice, lakes, and rivers. The remaining 29% of Earth's surface is <a href="/wiki/Land" title="Land">land</a>, consisting of continents and islands. Earth's
    surface layer is formed of several slowly moving <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plates</a>, interacting to produce mountain ranges, volcanoes, and earthquakes. Earth's liquid
    <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" class="mw-redirect" title="Outer core">outer core</a> generates the magnetic field that shapes Earth's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>, deflecting destructive
    <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar winds</a>.
</p>
<p>
    <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> consists mostly of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and
    <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. More solar energy is received by tropical regions than polar regions and is redistributed by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric</a> and
    <a href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean circulation">ocean circulation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">Water vapor</a> is widely present in the atmosphere and
    <a href="/wiki/Cloud_formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloud formation">forms clouds</a> that cover most of the planet. <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">Greenhouse gases</a> in the
    atmosphere like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) trap a part of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">energy from the Sun</a> close to the surface. A
    region's climate is governed by latitude, but also by elevation and proximity to moderating oceans. Severe weather, such as tropical cyclones, thunderstorms, and heatwaves, occurs in most areas and greatly impacts life.
</p>
<p>
    <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid" title="Earth ellipsoid">Earth is an ellipsoid</a> with <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_circumference" title="Earth&#39;s circumference">a circumference</a> of about 40,000&#160;km. It is the
    <a href="/wiki/List_of_Solar_System_objects_by_size" title="List of Solar System objects by size">densest planet in the Solar System</a>. Of the four
    <a href="/wiki/Rocky_planet" class="mw-redirect" title="Rocky planet">rocky planets</a>, it is the largest and most massive. Earth is about eight
    <a href="/wiki/Light_minute" class="mw-redirect" title="Light minute">light minutes</a> away from the Sun and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth&#39;s orbit">orbits it</a>, taking a year (about 365.25 days) to complete one
    revolution. <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth&#39;s rotation">Earth rotates</a> around its own axis in just less than a day (in about 23 hours and 56 minutes).
    <a href="#Axial_tilt_and_seasons">Earth's axis of rotation</a> is tilted with respect to the perpendicular to its orbital plane around the Sun, producing seasons. Earth is orbited by one
    <a href="/wiki/Claimed_moons_of_Earth" title="Claimed moons of Earth">permanent</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural satellite</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, which orbits Earth at
    380,000&#160;km (1.3 light seconds) and is roughly a quarter as wide as Earth. The Moon always faces the Earth with the same side through <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">tidal locking</a> and causes
    <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>, stabilizes Earth's axis, and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">gradually slows its rotation</a>.
</p>
<p>
    Earth formed <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Earth" title="Age of Earth">over 4.5 billion years ago</a>. During the first <a href="/wiki/Billion_years" title="Billion years">billion years</a> of
    <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s history">Earth's history</a>, the ocean formed and then <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">life developed</a> within it. Life spread globally and
    began to affect Earth's atmosphere and surface, leading to Earth's <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxidation_Event" title="Great Oxidation Event">Great Oxidation Event</a> two billion years ago.
    <a href="/wiki/Humans" class="mw-redirect" title="Humans">Humans</a> emerged 300,000 years ago, and have reached a population of almost 8 billion today. Humans depend on Earth's
    <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and natural resources for their survival, but have
    <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">increasingly impacted Earth's environment</a>. Today, humanity's impact on Earth's climate, soils, waters, and ecosystems is
    <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">unsustainable</a>, threatening people's lives and <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">causing widespread extinction of other life</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>
    The <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">modern English</a> word <span class="anchor" id="Name"></span><span class="anchor" id="Etymology"></span> <i>Earth</i> developed, via
    <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, from an <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> noun most often spelled
    <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eor%C3%B0e" class="extiw" title="wikt:eorðe">eorðe</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-oedearth_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedearth-31">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> It has cognates in every
    <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic language</a>, and their <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Germanic">ancestral root</a> has been reconstructed as
    <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/er%C3%BE%C5%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/erþō">*<i>erþō</i></a>. In its earliest attestation, the word <i>eorðe</i> was already being used to
    translate the many senses of <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terra" class="extiw" title="wikt:terra">terra</a></i> and
    <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Greek</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%E1%BF%86" class="extiw" title="wikt:γῆ">γῆ</a> <i>gē</i>: the ground, its
    <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, dry land, the human world, the surface of the world (including the sea), and the globe itself. As with Roman
    <a href="/wiki/Terra_(goddess)" class="mw-redirect" title="Terra (goddess)">Terra</a>/Tellūs and Greek <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(goddess)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaia (goddess)">Gaia</a>, Earth may have been a
    <a href="/wiki/Earth_goddess" title="Earth goddess">personified goddess</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germanic_religion_(aboriginal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic religion (aboriginal)">Germanic paganism</a>: late
    <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a> included <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Jörð">Jörð</a> ('Earth'), a giantess often given as the mother of <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>.

</p>
<p>
    Historically, <i>earth</i> has been written in lowercase. From <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Middle English">early Middle English</a>, its
    <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite sense</a> as "the globe" was expressed as <i>the</i> earth. By
    <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a>, many nouns were capitalized, and <i>the earth</i> was also written <i>the Earth</i>, particularly when referenced along with other heavenly
    bodies. More recently, the name is sometimes simply given as <i>Earth</i>, by analogy with the names of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">other planets</a>, though <i>earth</i> and forms with <i>the</i> remain
    common.<sup id="cite_ref-oedearth_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedearth-31">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Style_guide" title="Style guide">House styles</a> now vary:
    <a href="/wiki/Oxford_spelling" title="Oxford spelling">Oxford spelling</a> recognizes the lowercase form as the most common, with the capitalized form an acceptable variant. Another convention capitalizes "Earth" when appearing as a
    name (for example, "Earth's atmosphere") but writes it in lowercase when preceded by <i>the</i> (for example, "the atmosphere of the earth"). It almost always appears in lowercase in colloquial expressions such as "what on earth are you
    doing?"<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-33">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7017143271504000000♠"></span>4.54<span style="margin-left: 0.3em; margin-right: 0.15em;">±</span>0.04&#160;Ga</span> the primordial Earth had formed.
    <sup id="cite_ref-age_earth1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-age_earth1-43">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The bodies in
    <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">the Solar System formed and evolved</a> with the Sun. In theory, a
    <a href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a> partitions a volume out of a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular cloud</a> by gravitational collapse, which begins to
    spin and flatten into a <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_disk" class="mw-redirect" title="Circumstellar disk">circumstellar disk</a>, and then the planets grow out of that disk with the Sun. A nebula contains gas, ice grains, and
    <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a>). According to
    <a href="/wiki/Nebular_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Nebular theory">nebular theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a> formed by
    <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accretion</a>, with the primordial Earth being estimated as likely taking anywhere from 70 to 100 million years to form.

    <sup id="cite_ref-canup_asphaug2001b_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canup_asphaug2001b-47">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Between approximately
    4.1 and <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7017119918880000000♠"></span>3.8&#160;Ga</span>, numerous <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">asteroid impacts</a> during the
    <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">Late Heavy Bombardment</a> caused significant changes to the greater surface environment of the Moon and, by inference, to that of Earth.
    <sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_history">Geological history</span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geological_history_of_Earth" title="Geological history of Earth">Geological history of Earth</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
    <div class="thumbinner" style="width: 302px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Archean.png" class="image">
            <img
                alt=""
                src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Archean.png/300px-Archean.png"
                decoding="async"
                width="300"
                height="134"
                class="thumbimage"
                srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Archean.png/450px-Archean.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Archean.png/600px-Archean.png 2x"
                data-file-width="2510"
                data-file-height="1124"
            />
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
            <div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Archean.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
            Artist's impression of earth during the <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> eon, showing falling <a href="/wiki/Meteor" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteor">meteor</a>, erupting
            <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>, round <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">stromatolites</a>, and barren landscape
        </div>
    </div>
</div>
<p>
    <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> were formed by
    <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_activity" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic activity">volcanic activity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a>.
    <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Water vapor from these sources <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth" title="Origin of water on Earth">condensed</a> into the oceans, augmented by
    water and ice from asteroids, <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">protoplanets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>.
    <sup id="cite_ref-watersource_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-watersource-51">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Sufficient water to fill the oceans may have been on Earth since it formed.
    <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In this model, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> kept the oceans from freezing when the newly
    forming Sun <a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">had only 70%</a> of its <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">current luminosity</a>.
    <sup id="cite_ref-asp2002_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asp2002-53">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> By <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7017110451600000000♠"></span>3.5&#160;Ga</span>,
    <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth&#39;s magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> was established, which helped prevent the atmosphere from being stripped away by the
    <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-physorg20100304_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physorg20100304-54">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    As the molten outer layer of Earth cooled it <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">formed</a> the first solid <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth&#39;s crust">crust</a>, which is thought to have been
    <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> in composition. The first <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>, which was more <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a> in composition,
    formed by the partial melting of this mafic crust. The presence of grains of the <a href="/wiki/Hadean_zircon" title="Hadean zircon">mineral zircon of Hadean age</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eoarchean" title="Eoarchean">Eoarchean</a>
    <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> suggests that at least some felsic crust existed as early as <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7017138853440000000♠"></span>4.4&#160;Ga</span>,
    only <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002140000000000000♠"></span>140&#160;<a href="/wiki/Megaannum" class="mw-redirect" title="Megaannum">Ma</a></span> after Earth's formation.
    <sup id="cite_ref-science310_5756_1947_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-science310_5756_1947-55">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> There are two main models of how this initial small volume of continental crust evolved to reach its
    current abundance:<sup id="cite_ref-williams_santosh2004_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-williams_santosh2004-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> (1) a relatively steady growth up to the present day,
    <sup id="cite_ref-science164_1229_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-science164_1229-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> which is supported by the radiometric dating of continental crust globally and (2) an initial rapid growth in the
    volume of continental crust during the <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a>, forming the bulk of the continental crust that now exists,
    <sup id="cite_ref-ajes38_613_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ajes38_613-58">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tp322_19_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tp322_19-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> which is supported
    by isotopic evidence from <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> in sedimentary rocks. The two models and
    the data that support them can be reconciled by large-scale <a href="/wiki/Crustal_recycling" title="Crustal recycling">recycling of the continental crust</a>, particularly during the early stages of Earth's history.
    <sup id="cite_ref-Dhuime_etal_2018_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dhuime_etal_2018-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    New continental crust forms as a result of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, a process ultimately driven by the continuous loss of heat from Earth's interior. Over
    <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">the period</a> of hundreds of millions of years, tectonic forces have caused areas of continental crust to group together to form
    <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinents</a> that have subsequently broken apart. At approximately <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7016236682000000000♠"></span>750&#160;Ma</span>, one of the
    earliest known supercontinents, <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a>, began to break apart. The continents later recombined to form <a href="/wiki/Pannotia" title="Pannotia">Pannotia</a> at
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7016189345600000000♠"></span>600–540&#160;Ma</span>, then finally <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, which also began to break apart at
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7015568036800000000♠"></span>180&#160;Ma</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-bradley_2011_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradley_2011-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    The most recent pattern of <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> began about <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7015126230400000000♠"></span>40&#160;Ma</span>,
    <sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and then intensified during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> about
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7013946728000000000♠"></span>3&#160;Ma</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
    <a href="/wiki/High_latitude" class="mw-redirect" title="High latitude">High-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitude" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle latitude">middle-latitude</a> regions have since undergone repeated cycles of
    glaciation and thaw, repeating about every 21,000, 41,000 and 100,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-psc_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psc-64">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The
    <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Period" title="Last Glacial Period">Last Glacial Period</a>, colloquially called the "last ice age", covered large parts of the continents, up to the middle latitudes, in ice and ended about 11,700 years ago.
    <sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>
    <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">Chemical reactions</a> led to the first self-replicating molecules about four billion years ago. A half billion years later, the
    <a href="/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor" title="Last universal common ancestor">last common ancestor of all current life</a> arose.
    <sup id="cite_ref-sa282_6_90_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sa282_6_90-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The evolution of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> allowed the Sun's energy to be
    harvested directly by life forms. The resultant <a href="/wiki/Molecular_oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular oxygen">molecular oxygen</a> (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1060974363" />
    <span class="chemf nowrap">O<sub class="template-chem2-sub">2</sub></span>) accumulated in the atmosphere and due to interaction with ultraviolet solar radiation, formed a protective
    <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1060974363" /><span class="chemf nowrap">O<sub class="template-chem2-sub">3</sub></span>) in the
    upper atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20131003_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131003-67">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The incorporation of smaller cells within larger ones resulted in the
    <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Endosymbiotic theory">development of complex cells</a> called <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>.
    <sup id="cite_ref-jas22_3_225_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jas22_3_225-68">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> True multicellular organisms formed as cells within
    <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonies</a> became increasingly specialized. Aided by the absorption of harmful
    <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> by the ozone layer, life colonized Earth's surface.
    <sup id="cite_ref-burton20021129_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burton20021129-69">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Among the earliest <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> evidence for
    <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> is <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> fossils found in 3.48&#160;billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> in
    <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AST-20131108_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AST-20131108-70">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
    <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> found in 3.7&#160;billion-year-old
    <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" class="mw-redirect" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary</a> rocks in <a href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a>,
    <sup id="cite_ref-NG-20131208_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20131208-71">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and remains of <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic material</a> found in
    4.1&#160;billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-AP-20151019_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151019-72">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-PNAS-20151014-pdf_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-20151014-pdf-73">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The
    <a href="/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms" title="Earliest known life forms">earliest direct evidence of life</a> on Earth is contained in 3.45 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> rocks showing
    fossils of <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WU-20171218_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WU-20171218-74">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-PNAS-2017_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-2017-75">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    During the <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>, <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7016315576000000000♠"></span>1000 to 539&#160;Ma</span>, much of Earth might have been covered in ice. This
    hypothesis has been termed "<a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a>", and it is of particular interest because it preceded the
    <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a>, when multicellular life forms significantly increased in complexity.
    <sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Following the Cambrian explosion,
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7016168833160000000♠"></span>535&#160;Ma</span>, there have been at least five major <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinctions</a> and many minor ones.
    <sup id="cite_ref-sci215_4539_1501_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sci215_4539_1501-78">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-Stanley_2016_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley_2016-79">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Apart from the proposed current
    <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a> event, the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">most recent</a> was
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7015208280160000000♠"></span>66&#160;Ma</span>, when <a href="/wiki/Chicxulub_impactor" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicxulub impactor">an asteroid impact</a> triggered the extinction of the
    non-avian <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> and other large reptiles, but largely spared small animals such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>,
    <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. Mammalian life has diversified over the past <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7001660000000000000♠"></span>66&#160;Mys</span>,
    and several million years ago an African ape gained the ability to stand upright.<sup id="cite_ref-gould1994_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gould1994-80">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> This facilitated tool use and encouraged
    communication that provided the nutrition and stimulation needed for a larger brain, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Human_evolution" title="Human evolution">evolution of humans</a>. The
    <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">development of agriculture</a>, and then <a href="/wiki/List_of_ancient_civilizations" class="mw-redirect" title="List of ancient civilizations">civilization</a>, led
    to humans having an <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">influence on Earth</a> and the nature and quantity of other life forms that continues to this day.
    <sup id="cite_ref-bgsa119_1_140_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bgsa119_1_140-81">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Earth" title="Future of Earth">Future of Earth</a></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_catastrophic_risk" title="Global catastrophic risk">Global catastrophic risk</a></div>
<p>
    Because <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub style="font-size: 80%; vertical-align: -0.35em;">2</sub>) has a long lifespan in the atmosphere, moderate human CO
    <sub style="font-size: 80%; vertical-align: -0.35em;">2</sub> emissions may postpone the next glacial inception by 100,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    Earth's expected long-term future is tied to that of the Sun. Over the next <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000110000000000000♠"></span>1.1&#160;billion years</span>, solar luminosity will increase by 10%, and over the next
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000350000000000000♠"></span>3.5&#160;billion years</span> by 40%.<sup id="cite_ref-sun_future_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun_future-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Earth's
    increasing surface temperature will accelerate the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate%E2%80%93silicate_cycle" title="Carbonate–silicate cycle">inorganic carbon cycle</a>, reducing
    <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1060974363" /><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<sub class="template-chem2-sub">2</sub></span> concentration to levels lethally low for plants (
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="6995099999999999999♠"></span>10&#160;<a href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts per million">ppm</a></span> for
    <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4 photosynthesis</a>) in approximately <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7002100000000000000♠"></span>100–900&#160;million years</span>.
    <sup id="cite_ref-britt2000_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britt2000-84">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pnas1_24_9576_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pnas1_24_9576-85">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> The lack of
    vegetation will result in the loss of oxygen in the atmosphere, making animal life impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-ward_brownlee2002_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward_brownlee2002-86">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Due to the
    increased luminosity, Earth's mean temperature may reach 100&#160;°C (212&#160;°F) in 1.5 billion years, and all ocean water will evaporate and be lost to space, which may trigger a
    <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a>, within an estimated 1.6 to 3 billion years.
    <sup id="cite_ref-:2_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-87">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Even if the Sun were stable, a fraction of the water in the modern oceans will descend to the
    <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, due to reduced steam venting from mid-ocean ridges.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-87">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-hess5_4_569_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hess5_4_569-88">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    The Sun will <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">evolve</a> to become a <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> in about
    <span class="nowrap"><span data-sort-value="7000500000000000000♠"></span>5&#160;billion years</span>. Models predict that the Sun will expand to roughly 1&#160;
    <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (150&#160;million&#160;km; 93&#160;million&#160;mi), about 250 times its present radius.
    <sup id="cite_ref-sun_future_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun_future-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-sun_future_schroder_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun_future_schroder-89">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Earth's fate is less clear. As a red giant, the Sun will lose roughly 30% of its mass, so, without tidal
    effects, Earth will move to an orbit 1.7&#160;AU (250&#160;million&#160;km; 160&#160;million&#160;mi) from the Sun when the star reaches its maximum radius, otherwise, with tidal effects, it may enter the Sun's atmosphere and be
    vaporized.<sup id="cite_ref-sun_future_83-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun_future-83">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_shape">Size and shape</span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">Figure of the Earth</a></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />

</div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" />

<p>
    The shape of Earth is nearly spherical. There is a small flattening at the poles and <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">bulging</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> due to
    <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth&#39;s rotation">Earth's rotation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-milbert_smith96_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milbert_smith96-90">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, a better
    approximation of Earth's shape is an <a href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" class="mw-redirect" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate spheroid</a>, whose equatorial diameter is 43 kilometers (27&#160;mi) larger than the
    <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">pole</a>-to-pole diameter.<sup id="cite_ref-ngdc2006_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ngdc2006-91">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    The average diameter of the reference spheroid is 12,742 kilometers (7,918&#160;mi). Local <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> deviates from this idealized spheroid, although on a global scale these deviations
    are small compared to Earth's radius: the maximum deviation of only 0.17% is at the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a> (10,925 meters or 35,843 feet below local sea level),
    <sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> whereas <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> (8,848 meters or 29,029 feet above local sea level) represents a
    deviation of 0.14%.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;n 6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> The point on the surface farthest from
    Earth's <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> is the summit of the equatorial <a href="/wiki/Chimborazo_(volcano)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chimborazo (volcano)">Chimborazo</a> volcano in
    <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> (6,384.4&#160;km or 3,967.1&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-ps20_5_16_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ps20_5_16-96">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-lancet365_9462_831_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lancet365_9462_831-97">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
    <sup id="cite_ref-The_&#39;Highest&#39;_Spot_on_Earth_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_&#39;Highest&#39;_Spot_on_Earth-98">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p>
    In <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a>, the exact shape that Earth's oceans would adopt in the absence of land and perturbations such as tides and winds is called the <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a>. More
    precisely, the geoid is the surface of gravitational equipotential at <a href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a> (MSL).
    <sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_topography" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea surface topography">Sea surface topography</a> are water deviations from MSL,
    analogous to land topography.
</p>
